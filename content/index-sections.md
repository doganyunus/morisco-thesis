## SECTION 2 — CONTEMPORARY BRIDGE: "History That Lives Today"

**Opening pull quote:**
> "In 2015, Spain offered citizenship to descendants of Sephardic Jews expelled in 1492. No comparable gesture was extended to the descendants of the Moriscos."

**Section heading:** History That Lives Today

### Card 1 — The Refugee Question
Between 1609 and 1614, approximately 300,000 Moriscos were expelled from Spain and dispersed across the Mediterranean — to North Africa, France, Italy, and the Ottoman Empire. They left with only what they could carry; their property was confiscated.

Their story echoes in contemporary debates about refugee integration, religious minorities in Europe, and the political consequences of forced displacement.

### Card 2 — Fear of the "Other"
The *limpieza de sangre* (purity of blood) statutes excluded Moriscos from public office on grounds of ancestry. They were suspected of crypto-Islam — of being a "fifth column" inside Christian Spain, secretly loyal to the Ottoman enemy.

The language is centuries old, but the logic persists: in fears about Muslim communities in Western Europe, in debates about the proposed mosque in Granada, in the policing of "parallel societies."

### Card 3 — Interconnected Worlds
Europe, Ottoman Asia, and North Africa were not separate civilisations in the sixteenth century — they were entangled. The Habsburg and Ottoman Empires competed for the Mediterranean; their politics were inseparable; their intelligence services read each other's mail.

Contemporary Mediterranean migration crises, EU relations with Turkey and North Africa, and the geopolitics of the Strait of Gibraltar all have roots in this period.

### Card 4 — Empire and State-Building
The sixteenth century was the crucible of the modern state. Habsburg Spain, the Ottoman Empire, and the Italian city-states were competing laboratories of sovereignty — experimenting with borders, citizenship, religious uniformity, and bureaucratic control.

The questions they wrestled with — who belongs? who is loyal? who may stay? — remain the questions of our time.

**Bridge sentence:** This dissertation asks: how did the Moriscos navigate this world? What strategies did they use? And what does their story tell us about our own?

## SECTION 3 — HISTORICAL CONTEXT: "Who Were the Moriscos?"

### Timeline

| Year | Event |
|------|-------|
| 1492 | Fall of Granada; Capitulations of Granada guarantee Muslim freedom of religion |
| 1499 | Cardinal Cisneros enforces mass conversions in Granada; Albaicín rebellion |
| 1502 | Royal decree: all Castilian Muslims must convert or leave Spain |
| 1526 | Charles V decrees forced conversion in Aragon and Valencia |
| 1556 | Philip II takes the throne — persecution intensifies |
| 1567 | *Pragmática*: Arabic banned, manuscripts confiscated and destroyed, Arabic names prohibited |
| 1568 | Alpujarras Rebellion begins; Moriscos appeal to Kılıç Ali Pasha in Algiers |
| 1609 | Edict of Expulsion signed by Philip III, 22 September |
| 1614 | Final expulsions complete — approximately 300,000 people displaced |

### Text Block 1 — The Mudejars
Before forced conversion, Spain's Muslims were called *Mudejars* (from Arabic *mudajjan*, "permitted to remain"). Living under Christian rule, they retained their mosques, their language, and some cultural autonomy — guaranteed by the Capitulations of Granada that ended the Reconquista in 1492. That guarantee did not survive one decade.

### Text Block 2 — The New Christians
Converted Muslims became *nuevos convertidos de moros* — New Christians of Moorish origin. By mid-century the word *Morisco* was in common use. The Spanish Inquisition monitored their religious practice and prosecuted suspected crypto-Muslims. The *limpieza de sangre* (purity of blood) statutes excluded them from offices, guilds, and honours on grounds of ancestry alone.

### Text Block 3 — Between Two Faiths
Many Moriscos maintained Islamic practice in secret — crypto-Muslims who used *taqiyya* (dissimulation of faith, permitted under Islamic law when under compulsion) to justify outward compliance with Christianity. They preserved elements of Andalusi culture through *Aljamiado* — Spanish written in Arabic script — and through music, diet, dress, and prayer conducted behind closed doors.

### Contextual image
Pere Oromig, *Embarco moriscos en el Grao de Valencia* (1616). Colección Bancaja, Valencia. One of a series of contemporary paintings commissioned to document the expulsion.

## SECTION 5 — CHAPTER I: "Setting the Stage (1492–1568)"

**Badge:** (no strategy badge — introductory chapter)
**Image:** Piri Reis, coastline of Andalusia, from *Kitâbü'l-Bahrîye* (Book of Navigation), 16th century

The story begins not with the expulsion but with its slow, century-long preparation. When Cardinal Cisneros arrived in Granada in 1499 and tore up the Capitulations of Granada — the treaty that had guaranteed Muslim freedom of religion — he set in motion a process that would end with 300,000 people at the waterside, looking back at their homeland.

This chapter traces the transformation of Spain's Muslim communities from Mudejars (subjects permitted to remain) to Moriscos (New Christians under Inquisitorial watch), and documents the first, fragile attempts to seek help from beyond the Strait of Gibraltar — from the Marinids of Morocco, the Mamluks of Egypt, and finally the rising power of Ottoman Istanbul.

**Pull quote:**
> "When Granada's hour of need came, no assistance was forthcoming from any quarter whatsoever."
> — L.P. Harvey, *Muslims in Spain, 1500 to 1614*

## SECTION 6 — CHAPTER II: "Between Empire and Rebellion (1568–1580)"

**Strategy badge:** Diplomacy
**Image:** The Alpujarras mountains south of Granada

On Christmas Eve 1568, the Moriscos of the Alpujarras mountains south of Granada rose in rebellion. Led by Aben Humeya — who proclaimed himself King of the Moriscos and Moors of Granada — the uprising was desperate and ultimately doomed. Don Juan of Austria suppressed it in 1571, and approximately 250,000 Granadans were forcibly relocated into Castile.

But before and during the rebellion, Morisco envoys were making a different kind of argument in Istanbul. In 1577, delegations from Valencia and Aragon reached Grand Vizier Sokollu Mehmed Pasha and Grand Admiral Kılıç Ali Pasha, offering to fund an Ottoman fleet along the Spanish coast. This chapter reconstructs that 1577 Istanbul mission — the most ambitious Morisco diplomatic enterprise of the sixteenth century — from Ottoman and Spanish intelligence sources.

**Pull quote:**
> "The Moriscos of Valencia and Aragon sent envoys to Istanbul in 1577, bearing gifts — and offering to fund an Ottoman fleet."
> — Yunus Doğan, *Moriscos and the Ottoman Empire*

## SECTION 7 — CHAPTER III: "Networks of Trust: Spies and Brokers (1568–1609)"

**Strategy badge:** Intelligence
**Image:** Cesare Ripa, "The Spy," *Nova Iconologia* (1618)

While diplomacy failed to produce an Ottoman fleet, individual Moriscos found a different way to make themselves valuable to Istanbul: intelligence. This chapter maps the Morisco role in Ottoman espionage networks across the Mediterranean — tracking the careers of Francisco Mariano and Jerónimo de Amiqui, two Morisco agents whose movements are reconstructed across thousands of pages of Spanish, Venetian, and Ottoman archives.

The Moriscos who served as intelligence brokers were genuinely trans-imperial figures — some reported to Istanbul, some to Madrid, some to both simultaneously. Their bicultural knowledge was their most valuable asset: they could move between Christian and Muslim worlds, speak both languages, read both codes.

**Pull quote:**
> "Their trans-imperial identities were not weaknesses to hide — they were tools to deploy."
> — Yunus Doğan, *Moriscos and the Ottoman Empire*

## SECTION 8 — CHAPTER IV: "Crossing the Mediterranean (1609–1614)"

**Strategy badge:** Mobility
**Image:** Joan Riezo, portolan chart of the Mediterranean Sea (1590)

When the Edict of Expulsion arrived in September 1609, the Morisco diaspora was already in motion. Ottoman envoy Hajı Ibrahim Müteferrika — himself of Morisco origin — arrived in Venice and addressed the Doge's council in Spanish, without a translator, on behalf of the Sultan. Letters concealed in fishing nets on sponge boats linked Moriscos in Marseille with their counterparts in Istanbul.

This chapter traces the three main exile routes: the western route from Valencia to Oran and Algiers; the central route through Marseille, Toulon, and Genoa to Venice; and the eastern route through Ragusa to Istanbul. At each node, Morisco agents negotiated passage, protection, and settlement — making movement itself into a form of political action.

**Three routes:**
1. **Western:** Spain → Oran / Algiers
2. **Central:** Spain → France → Italy → Venice
3. **Eastern:** Venice → Ragusa → Istanbul

**Pull quote:**
> "Movement was not merely flight — it was strategy."
> — Yunus Doğan, *Moriscos and the Ottoman Empire*

## SECTION 9 — CHAPTER V: "A New Life in Istanbul (1609–1620s)"

**Strategy badge:** Conversion & Identity
**Image:** Braun & Hogenberg, *Byzantium Nunc Constantinopolis* (1572), detail showing Galata

Arriving in Istanbul dressed "half Spanish and half Turkish" — as a Venetian bailo reported — the Moriscos who reached the Ottoman capital confronted a new question: who were they now? They had survived by performing Christianity in Spain. In Istanbul, they were expected to perform Islam. But Ottoman authorities were not always convinced by their reconversions, and the Moriscos' Western dress, Spanish language, and Catholic habits made them objects of curiosity and suspicion in equal measure.

This chapter follows Morisco settlement in the Galata district — the cosmopolitan quarter across the Golden Horn where Genoese, Greeks, Armenians, Jews, and now Moriscos lived in uneasy proximity — and examines how figures like Ahmad b. Qasim al-Hajarī built careers as intermediaries between the Ottoman, European, and North African worlds.

**Pull quote:**
> "Dressed half Spanish, half Turkish — the Moriscos embodied the in-betweenness that defined Mediterranean life."
> — Yunus Doğan, after Simon Contarini, Venetian bailo report, c. 1610

## SECTION 10 — CHAPTER VI: "Settling the Stateless"

**Strategy badge:** (none — concluding chapter)
**Image:** Historical view of Algiers harbor

The Morisco diaspora was not simply received by the Ottoman Empire — it was directed. Ottoman imperial policy assigned Morisco settlers to specific locations across the empire: North Africa (Morocco, Tunisia, Algeria), Anatolia, the Balkans, and the Aegean islands. Sultan Ahmed I's 1613 decree to the cadi of Salonica ordered the settlement and protection of incoming Andalusians.

This final chapter traces the long aftermath of the expulsion — the Morisco communities that took root in Salé and Rabat (where Hornachero Moriscos built a short-lived republic), in Tunis, in Istanbul's Galata, in Salonica, and across the Anatolian interior. It asks what "home" meant for a community defined by its displacement, and how the Ottoman state used the Moriscos even as it sought to protect them.

**Pull quote:**
> "The Ottomans did not merely receive the Moriscos — they directed them, settled them, and made them subjects."
> — Yunus Doğan, *Moriscos and the Ottoman Empire*

## SECTION 11 — FOUR STRATEGIES: "How the Moriscos Survived"

**Section heading:** How the Moriscos Survived
**Section subtitle:** Four strategies across a century of displacement

### Strategy 1 — Diplomacy
Icon: compass/navigation circle
Envoys to Istanbul, Paris, Venice, and London. A stateless people negotiating across imperial frontiers without recognised sovereignty — using gifts, letters, petitions, and personal networks to seek protection from the most powerful rulers of the age.

### Strategy 2 — Intelligence
Icon: eye
Morisco spies across the Mediterranean — serving Ottoman and Spanish sides alike. Their bicultural knowledge — two languages, two religions, two sets of contacts — made them the most effective intelligence brokers of the sixteenth-century Mediterranean.

### Strategy 3 — Mobility
Icon: wave / sea
From Granada to Algiers, Marseille to Venice to Istanbul — movement itself as a political strategy, not mere flight. Moriscos used their mobility to escape persecution, build new networks, and negotiate from positions of leverage across multiple courts simultaneously.

### Strategy 4 — Conversion
Icon: crescent
Public Christianity, private Islam — and the complex remaking of identity after expulsion. *Taqiyya* (dissimulation of faith permitted under compulsion) was not weakness but strategy. After 1609, the question reversed: how to perform Islam convincingly in Ottoman Istanbul after a lifetime of performing Christianity?

## SECTION 12 — KEY FIGURES

**Section heading:** Key Historical Figures
**Section subtitle:** The people at the centre of this history

### Row 1 — Morisco Leaders and Diplomats

**Ahmad b. Qasim al-Hajarī** · c. 1570–1640
Scholar and diplomat; author of *Kitab Nasir al-Din ʿala al-Qawm al-Kafirin*; Morisco intellectual who moved between Morocco, Europe, and the Ottoman world. He converted in Spain, fled after the expulsion, served in Morocco, and traveled widely as an advocate for Morisco causes.

**Aben Humeya** · d. 1569
Born Fernando de Córdoba y Válor; proclaimed himself King of the Moriscos and Moors of Granada. Led the Alpujarras Rebellion from December 1568; assassinated by rivals within his own movement before Don Juan of Austria's final suppression in 1571.

**Jerónimo Enríquez** · c. 1610–1615
Morisco procurator based in Marseille during the years of the expulsion. Key coordinator of diaspora communication networks linking Morisco communities in Spain, France, and Istanbul; his correspondence survives in the Archivo General de Simancas.

**Hajı Ibrahim Müteferrika** · fl. 1609
Ottoman envoy of Morisco origin, sent by Sultan Ahmed I to Venice in 1609. Addressed the Venetian Collegio in Spanish without a translator on behalf of the Sultan, requesting Venetian assistance for Morisco exiles. A remarkable example of the Ottoman use of Morisco cultural capital.

### Row 2 — Ottoman and Habsburg Figures

**Sokollu Mehmed Pasha** · 1506–1579
Grand Vizier of the Ottoman Empire from 1565 to 1579. Bosnia-born devşirme who rose through the palace system to dominate Ottoman policy for over a decade. Received the Morisco delegations of 1577 and was the central figure in Ottoman Mediterranean strategy; survived three sultans.

**Kılıç Ali Pasha** · c. 1500–1587
Grand Admiral (Kapudan Pasha) of the Ottoman fleet. Born Giovanni Dionigi Galeni in Le Castella, Calabria; captured by Barbary corsairs and converted to Islam. The principal Ottoman naval commander after Lepanto; his conversion trajectory made him a natural interlocutor for the Morisco cause.

**Sultan Selim II** · r. 1566–1574
Ottoman Sultan during whose reign the Cyprus campaign was launched, the Battle of Lepanto was fought (1571), and the first major Morisco petitions reached the Ottoman court. His reign marked the high point of Ottoman-Morisco diplomatic contact at the imperial level.

**Philip II** · r. 1556–1598
King of Spain, Sicily, and Portugal. Issued the *pragmática* of 1567 banning Arabic and Morisco cultural practices; his reign saw the Alpujarras Rebellion and the intensification of Inquisitorial pressure on Morisco communities. Father of Philip III, who signed the Edict of Expulsion.

## SECTION 14 — METHODOLOGY TEASER: "About the Research"

**Section heading:** About the Research

Reconstructing the Morisco experience required research in eleven archives across Spain, Italy, and Turkey — reading diplomatic correspondence in Spanish, Ottoman Turkish, Italian, French, and Latin; comparing inquisitorial records against Ottoman imperial registers; and tracing individual lives across thousands of pages of bureaucratic paper.

The dissertation integrates digital humanities methods: network analysis using Gephi to map relationships between Morisco agents, Ottoman patrons, and European intermediaries; and geospatial mapping using Carto to trace migration routes and settlement patterns.

**Archive teasers:**
1. AGS — Archivo General de Simancas — Valladolid, Spain
2. ASV — Archivio di Stato di Venezia — Venice, Italy
3. BOA — Başbakanlık Osmanlı Arşivleri — Istanbul, Turkey

## SECTION 15 — ABOUT THE RESEARCHER

**Section heading:** About the Researcher

Yunus Doğan is a historian of the early modern Mediterranean and a PhD candidate in History and Civilisation at the European University Institute in Florence, Italy. His dissertation examines the entangled histories of the Morisco diaspora and the Ottoman Empire across the Mediterranean from 1520 to 1620.

He has conducted archival research in eleven archives across Spain (Simancas, Madrid, Barcelona, Valencia), Italy (Venice, Florence, Mantua), Turkey (Istanbul), and France (Paris), working with primary sources in Spanish, Ottoman Turkish, Italian, French, Arabic, and Latin. He holds the Paul Oskar Kristeller Fellowship in Renaissance Studies.
