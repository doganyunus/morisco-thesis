# Sources and Methodology Content

## Methodology Overview

### Trans-Imperial Approach
The dissertation adopts a trans-imperial framework that situates Morisco history within the overlapping and competing structures of the Habsburg Spanish and Ottoman empires. Rather than studying the Moriscos from within a single national history, the research follows their networks across imperial frontiers — tracing the same individuals, families, and letters through Spanish, Italian, Ottoman, and French archives simultaneously.

### Microhistory
Alongside the structural analysis, the dissertation uses microhistorical close reading to reconstruct individual lives. The movements of Francisco Mariano (spy), Jerónimo Enríquez (Marseille procurator), and Ahmad b. Qasim al-Hajarī (scholar-diplomat) are traced in granular detail through bureaucratic paper trails, allowing the larger patterns of Morisco experience to emerge from specific, documented cases.

### Digital Humanities
The dissertation integrates digital humanities tools at two levels: network analysis (using Gephi) to map the relationships between Morisco agents, Ottoman patrons, and European intermediaries; and geospatial mapping (using Carto) to visualise Morisco migration routes and settlement patterns across the Mediterranean. Seven maps appear in the dissertation.

## Source Types

1. **Diplomatic Correspondence** — Ambassadors' reports, imperial orders, letters between courts. The Venetian bailo dispatches (SDC) and Spanish papeles de estado provide the richest documentation of Morisco-Ottoman contacts.

2. **Inquisitorial Records** — Trial sentences, proceeding records from the Spanish Inquisition. While biased sources, they preserve details about crypto-Islamic practices, networks, and identities that Moriscos themselves left no record of.

3. **Ottoman Imperial Registers** — The Mühimme Defterleri record imperial orders concerning Moriscos; these appear rarely but are decisive when they do, proving that Morisco affairs reached the highest levels of Ottoman government.

4. **Chronicles and Travel Accounts** — Luis del Mármol Carvajal's Historia del rebelión (1600); Evliya Çelebi's Seyahatnâme; Mustafa Selaniki's Tarih-i Selaniki provide narrative context.

5. **Morisco Intellectual Writings** — Ahmad b. Qasim al-Hajarī's Kitab Nasir al-Din ʿala al-Qawm al-Kafirin; Aljamiado manuscripts from the BNE. These rare first-person Morisco voices are treated with particular care.

## Glossary (35+ terms)

### People and Communities

**Morisco** — Former Muslim of the Iberian Peninsula forcibly converted to Christianity between 1499 and 1526.

**Mudéjar** — A Muslim who lived as a subject of a Christian kingdom before forced conversions; from Arabic *mudajjan*, "permitted to remain."

**Mudajjan / müdeccel / müdeccan** — Ottoman terminology for the Morisco community; transliterations of the Arabic root meaning "permitted to remain."

**Andalusī / Andalusian** — Arabic/Ottoman term for Moriscos settled in the Maghreb after expulsion; referring to their Iberian (al-Andalus) origin.

**Ehl-i Endülüs** — Ottoman term meaning "the people of al-Andalus"; used in imperial correspondence to designate expelled Moriscos.

**Taife-i müdeccel** — Ottoman term for the Morisco community as a collective group; *taife* denotes a community or group.

**New Christians (nuevos convertidos de moros)** — Official Spanish term for converted Muslims; distinguished from *conversos* (converted Jews).

**Converso / Judeoconverso** — Descendant of Jews who converted to Christianity, many under duress; subject to similar suspicions as Moriscos.

**Crypto-Muslim** — A Morisco who publicly practised Christianity while secretly maintaining Islamic faith and practice.

**Marrano** — Pejorative term for a Christian suspected of secretly practising Judaism; related phenomenon to crypto-Islam among Moriscos.

**Renegade** — A Christian-born convert to Islam in the Ottoman or North African context; Kılıç Ali Pasha is the most prominent example in this dissertation.

**Sephardic Jews / Sephardim** — Jews of Iberian origin expelled in 1492 (from Spain) and 1497 (from Portugal); often settled alongside Moriscos in Ottoman cities.

**Alfaquí** — Among Moriscos, the local religious leader who served as imam for crypto-Muslim communities; often the carrier of Aljamiado manuscripts.

**Dragoman** — Professional interpreter and cultural broker in the early modern Mediterranean; Moriscos frequently served in this capacity.

### Religion and Law

**Limpieza de sangre** — "Purity of blood" statutes excluding those of Jewish or Muslim ancestry from certain offices, institutions, and honours; core mechanism of Morisco exclusion.

**Taqiyya** — Islamic concept of permitted religious dissimulation under coercion; cited by North African jurists (in fatwas) as justification for Moriscos' outward Christian practice.

**Fetva / Fatwa** — A formal legal opinion issued by a qualified Islamic scholar; the Oran Fetva (c. 1504) is particularly important for legitimising Morisco crypto-Islam.

**Şeyhülislam** — The highest religious authority (grand mufti) in the Ottoman Empire; issued opinions on the status of Morisco Muslims.

**Inquisición / Inquisition** — The Spanish Inquisition; primary instrument of Morisco persecution; its records are a key source for this dissertation.

**Pragmática** — A Spanish royal decree; Philip II's 1567 pragmática banned Arabic language, Morisco dress, and cultural practices, precipitating the Alpujarras Rebellion.

**Reconquista** — The Christian military reconquest of the Iberian Peninsula from Muslim rulers; completed with the fall of Granada in 1492.

**Capitulations of Granada** — The 1492 treaty guaranteeing freedom of religion, property, and customs to Granada's Muslim population; broken by Cardinal Cisneros within seven years.

**Confessionalization** — The early modern process linking religious identity to political belonging and state formation; the Moriscos were victims of its Spanish implementation.

**Jihad** — Holy war; frequently invoked in Morisco-Ottoman diplomatic discourse as a framework for Ottoman intervention in Spain on behalf of persecuted Muslims.

### Ottoman Administration

**Sublime Porte (Bâb-ı Âlî)** — The office of the grand vizier; broadly, the central Ottoman court and government; used as a metonym for the Ottoman state.

**Divan** — The Ottoman imperial council of state; met four times weekly; the arena for Ottoman foreign policy decisions regarding Moriscos.

**Grand Vizier (Sadrazam)** — Chief minister of the Ottoman Empire; Sokollu Mehmed Pasha (1565–1579) is the key figure in this dissertation.

**Kapudan Pasha** — Grand Admiral of the Ottoman fleet; Kılıç Ali Pasha held this office from 1571 to 1587 and was the principal Ottoman link to Morisco petitioners.

**Beylerbeyi / Beylerbeylik** — Provincial governor / province in the Ottoman system; the beylerbeyis of Algiers and Tunis were key intermediaries with Morisco communities in North Africa.

**Kapı** — Household and patronage network of an Ottoman grandee; Morisco envoys sought to enter the *kapı* of Sokollu Mehmed Pasha and Kılıç Ali Pasha.

**Hükm** — An official imperial decree issued by the Ottoman Sultan; several *hükms* concerning Morisco settlers are recorded in the Mühimme Defterleri.

**Mühimme Defterleri** — Ottoman imperial registers of important orders; key archival source in the Başbakanlık Osmanlı Arşivleri (BOA); abbreviated MD.

**Devşirme** — The Ottoman system of recruiting boys from Christian families for imperial service; both Sokollu Mehmed Pasha and Kılıç Ali Pasha entered Ottoman service through related conversion processes.

### Diplomatic and Analytical Terms

**Trans-imperial** — Spanning or crossing multiple empires; the dissertation's central analytical framework for understanding Morisco agency.

**Trans-imperial subjects** — Individuals who crossed between empires, negotiating belonging across boundaries; Francisco Mariano and Jerónimo de Amiqui are exemplary cases.

**Glocal / Glocalisation** — The interaction between global imperial structures and local Morisco communities; used to analyse how local Morisco networks plugged into Ottoman and European imperial systems.

**Bailo** — The Venetian ambassador to the Ottoman court; the bailo's dispatches (Senato Dispacci Costantinopoli) are a major source for Morisco-Ottoman relations.

**Diaspora** — A community dispersed from its homeland while maintaining collective memory, cultural identity, and often aspirations of return.

**Cultural brokerage** — Mediating between different cultural, linguistic, or religious worlds; Moriscos were among the most effective cultural brokers in the early modern Mediterranean.

**In-betweenness** — The liminal condition of Moriscos: neither fully accepted as Christians in Spain nor fully trusted as Muslims in Ottoman lands; their strength lay precisely in this ambiguity.

**Corsair** — A licensed pirate or privateer; key figure in the early modern Mediterranean economy; Kılıç Ali Pasha began his career as a corsair.

**Microhistory** — Historical method focusing on small-scale case studies to illuminate larger structural patterns; associated with Carlo Ginzburg and Giovanni Levi.

**Prosopography** — Systematic study of collective biographies; used for network analysis of Morisco agents and their Ottoman and European contacts.

**Longue durée** — Fernand Braudel's concept of long-duration historical processes; the Mediterranean as a space of slow-changing structures underlying the event-history of the Morisco expulsion.
