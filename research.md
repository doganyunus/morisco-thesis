### Abstract (Author's Own)

This dissertation explores the entangled histories of the Moriscos and the Ottoman Empire across the Mediterranean from the sixteenth to the early seventeenth century. It traces the ways Morisco communities engaged with Ottoman socio-political, economic, religious, and cultural spheres through diverse forms of contact and exchange. Adopting a comparative and trans-imperial approach and drawing on archival research conducted in Spain, Italy, and Turkey, the study examines how Moriscos employed strategies of mobility, conversion, intelligence, and cultural brokerage to navigate the intertwined political and confessional landscapes of Europe and the Ottoman world. It argues that Morisco mobility and interaction redefined notions of sovereignty, faith, and belonging across imperial frontiers, contributing to broader debates on migration, displacement, and identity in a globalizing world.

### Core Thesis and Arguments

The dissertation makes three interconnected arguments:

1. **Trans-Imperial Mobility and Subjecthood:** The Moriscos exemplified how individuals moved between empires and negotiated political and legal identities across confessional boundaries. Their experiences of conversion, reconversion, and the performance of multiple religious identities reveal how faith was strategically mobilised in an era of confessionalization.

2. **Diplomacy Beyond State Actors:** Morisco diplomacy extended beyond official ambassadors to include displaced non-state actors who negotiated protection and legitimacy through petitions, alliances, cultural brokerage, and espionage. Their practice was "glocal"—simultaneously rooted in local pressures and operating across trans-imperial networks.

3. **Deconstruction of the Ottoman "Monolith":** Neither the Ottoman Empire nor the Morisco diaspora operated as unified actors. Ottoman foreign policy was shaped by competing household factions (kapı) — admirals, governors, and grand viziers with overlapping and sometimes conflicting agendas. Similarly, Morisco communities were internally diverse, with regional and factional interests that shaped their strategies.

## Part II: Historical Context

### Who Were the Moriscos?

**Origins — the Mudejars:** Before 1502, Spain's Muslims living under Christian rule were called *Mudejars* (from Arabic *mudajjan*, "permitted to remain"). They retained their religion, mosques, and some cultural autonomy under the terms of the Reconquista. The Capitulations of Granada (1492), which ended the Reconquista, initially guaranteed freedom of religion to Granada's Muslim population.

**Forced Conversion (1499–1526):** This balance collapsed in 1499 when Cardinal Francisco Jiménez de Cisneros arrived in Granada and enforced rapid conversions, sparking the Albaicín rebellion. The rebellion was suppressed by 1501, and in 1502 a royal decree required all Muslims in Castile to convert or leave. In the Crown of Aragon (Valencia), Muslims retained Mudejar status until 1526, when Charles V (r. 1519–1558) issued a decree mandating their conversion following the Germanías rebellion (1519–1522).

**The "New Christians":** Converted Muslims were referred to as *nuevos convertidos de moros* (New Christians of Moorish origin), and by the mid-sixteenth century the term *Morisco* had become standard. Many continued to practise Islam secretly — they are sometimes called *crypto-Muslims*. They maintained elements of Andalusi culture through language (Aljamiado — Spanish written in Arabic script), dress, diet, music, and religious practice.

**Persecution and the Inquisition:** Moriscos faced the *limpieza de sangre* (purity of blood) statutes, which excluded those of Muslim or Jewish ancestry from many offices and honours. The Spanish Inquisition monitored their religious practice and prosecuted suspected crypto-Muslims. Under Philip II (r. 1556–1598), a *pragmática* of 1567 intensified restrictions: Arabic dress was banned, Arabic manuscripts were confiscated and destroyed, Arabic names were prohibited.

**Regional Variations:** The Morisco population was not homogeneous. The Granadinos (from the former kingdom of Granada) were often more persistently attached to Islam; those of Valencia and Aragon (the largest regional groups) had a longer tradition of integration; Castilian Moriscos were more assimilated. These regional differences shaped their strategies and their relationships with Ottoman and North African powers.

---

### The Expulsion of the Moriscos

**Alpujarras Rebellion (1568–1571):** The immediate trigger for mass expulsion was the Morisco uprising in the Alpujarras mountains south of Granada, beginning Christmas Eve 1568. The rebellion was led by Aben Humeya (Fernando de Válor) and later Aben Aboo. It was suppressed by Don Juan of Austria in 1571, and approximately 250,000 Moriscos were forcibly relocated from Granada into Castile. The rebellion marked a turning point: it convinced Spanish authorities that peaceful assimilation was impossible.

**The Final Expulsion (1609–1614):** Under Philip III (r. 1598–1621), the Duke of Lerma's government issued the Edict of Expulsion on 22 September 1609. The expulsion was carried out in phases:
- Valencia (1609): approximately 120,000–130,000 expelled
- Aragon and Catalonia (1610)
- Castile, Murcia, and remaining populations (1610–1614)
- Total expelled: approximately 300,000 people

Moriscos were ordered to leave with only what they could carry; property was confiscated. Controversial provisions included the separation of children under five from their parents, with the youngest remaining in Spain.

**Destinations:** Most Moriscos went to North Africa (Morocco, Algeria, Tunisia). Others went to France, then dispersed further. Smaller groups reached Italy (Tuscany, Rome, Mantua), and eventually some reached the Ottoman Empire (Istanbul, Salonica, Anatolia, the Balkans). A minority remained in Spain under various pretexts or integrated into the Old Christian population.

---

### Early Morisco Connections to the Islamic World (Pre-1568)

**North African connections:** Granada maintained centuries-old ties with the Marinids of Morocco (based in Fez), the Zayyanids of Tlemcen, and the Hafsids of Tunis. By the late fifteenth century, these dynasties were too weakened by internal conflict and European pressure to provide effective military support. The 1535 treaty between Charles V and Hafsid ruler Muley al-Hassan even prohibited Moriscos from residing in Tunis.

**Mamluk appeals:** From the mid-fourteenth century, the Nasrids of Granada sent repeated diplomatic missions to Cairo seeking Mamluk military assistance. In 1440, Sultan Muhammad IX sent an envoy to the Mamluk Sultan al-Zahir Jaqmaq; the response was sympathetic but offered only modest financial support. During the Albaicín uprising of 1499–1500, residents sent appeals to the Sultan of Egypt. The Mamluks lacked the capacity and strategic interest for direct western Mediterranean intervention.

**Ottoman contacts:** As Ottoman power expanded in the Mediterranean, Nasrid-Ottoman contacts began. The fall of Constantinople (1453) and the Ottoman conquest of Egypt (1517) established the Ottomans as the dominant Islamic power. Andalusian hopes shifted eastward. From the 1520s onward, Moriscos increasingly sought Ottoman support, sending letters, delegations, and gifts to Istanbul.

## Part III: Key Themes

### 1. Trans-Imperial Diplomacy

The dissertation's central contribution is showing that Morisco political activity constituted a genuine, if unconventional, form of diplomacy. Without recognised sovereignty, Morisco representatives:
- Sent delegations bearing gifts to grand viziers, admirals, and the sultan himself
- Wrote letters to the Şeyhülislam requesting fatwas in their support
- Negotiated with Henry IV of France, the Dutch Republic, and England
- Leveraged Ottoman diplomatic pressure on Venice and France to facilitate their passage
- Acted as intermediaries between Christian and Muslim powers

The concept of **glocal diplomacy** is central: Morisco strategies were simultaneously local (responding to immediate pressures of expulsion, poverty, persecution) and trans-imperial (mobilising networks spanning Iberia, North Africa, Italy, France, and Istanbul).

### 2. Intelligence and Espionage

Chapter III reveals an extensive Morisco role in Ottoman intelligence networks:
- **Political intelligence:** Reporting on conditions in Spain, encouraging rebellion, assessing likelihood of further uprising
- **Military intelligence:** Mapping fortifications, tracking fleet movements, identifying coastal vulnerabilities
- **Covert plotting:** Organising or facilitating conspiracies against Spanish authorities
- **Recruitment and propaganda:** Expanding spy networks across the Mediterranean
- **Double agency:** Some Moriscos also served European (Spanish, Venetian) intelligence, making them genuinely trans-imperial actors

Key spy case studies include Francisco Mariano (tracked across the Mediterranean in Map 6) and Jerónimo de Amiqui (Map 7).

### 3. Religious Identity and Conversion

The Morisco experience challenges simple binaries of "Muslim" vs. "Christian":
- In Spain, they performed Catholicism while maintaining crypto-Islamic practices (prayer, fasting during Ramadan, ablution, Arabic names used in private)
- **Taqiyya** (dissimulation of faith permitted in Islamic law under compulsion) provided theological justification for outward compliance
- After expulsion, many "reconverted" to Islam publicly, but Ottoman authorities sometimes doubted their sincerity and imposed conditions
- Ottoman Istanbul presented its own challenges: Moriscos arriving in Western dress were perceived ambiguously — "half Spanish and half Turkish" as one Venetian bailo reported
- The dissertation analyses conversion not as a fixed event but as a continuous performance of identity shaped by political and social context

### 4. Diaspora Formation and Community Networks

The Morisco diaspora functioned through informal but functional trans-Mediterranean networks:
- **Before expulsion:** Merchants, spies, and envoys maintained contacts between Iberia, North Africa, and Istanbul
- **During expulsion (1609–1614):** A coordinated network linked Moriscos in Spain, Marseille, Venice, and Istanbul; letters were passed through intermediaries including Jewish merchants and French commanders
- **Community leadership:** Key families (Izquierdos, Compañeros, Zaydejos) and individuals (Jerónimo Enríquez in Marseille, Ali and Süleyman in Istanbul) coordinated collective action
- **Difference from Sephardic diaspora:** Unlike the Sephardic Jews, the Moriscos lacked formal communal institutions; their networks were fragile and left fewer paper trails

### 5. Mediterranean Trade Networks

The dissertation reveals commercial dimensions of Morisco activity:
- Moriscos used commercial networks to transfer money across borders (documented case: Gaspar de Raya working with a Genoese merchant in Madrid to transfer funds to Istanbul)
- Iberian Conversos and Christians transported Morisco goods to Mediterranean ports before and during expulsion
- Venice was a key transit point for goods and people; Moriscos sought to "sell their goods" before continuing east
- Florentine and Mantuan ducal authorities sought Morisco settlers as agricultural labourers
- Grand Duke Cosimo II de' Medici's early seventeenth-century project to resettle Moriscos in Tuscany

### 6. Cross-Cultural Exchange and Cultural Brokerage

**Dragomans and intermediaries:** The dissertation situates Moriscos within the broader Mediterranean world of translators, brokers, and go-betweens:
- Morisco intellectuals like Ahmad b. Qasim al-Hajarī (c. 1570–c.1640) moved between European and Islamic worlds, writing in both Arabic and European languages
- Morisco envoys in Istanbul could address the Venetian Collegio directly in Spanish (as Hajı Ibrahim Müteferrika did in 1609)
- Their bilingualism and bicultural knowledge made Moriscos valuable across confessional lines

**Cross-confessional collaboration:** Beyond Muslim-Christian boundaries:
- Moriscos worked with Sephardic Jews (documented network through Bayonne to Tunis and Venice)
- Moriscos enlisted under false names in Venetian militias, exchanging letters with Granadans in Istanbul through Jewish intermediaries concealed in fishing nets on sponge boats (1614)

## Part VII: Field-Specific Vocabulary and Terminology

This glossary is designed to inform the tone, labels, and navigation of a public-facing portfolio website. Terms are organised thematically.

### People and Communities

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Morisco** | Former Muslim of the Iberian Peninsula and their descendants, forcibly converted to Christianity between 1499 and 1526. The standard term in both historical scholarship and this dissertation. |
| **Mudéjar** (or Mudejar) | A Muslim who lived permanently as a subject of a Christian kingdom of the Iberian Peninsula before the forced conversions; from Arabic *mudajjan*, "permitted to remain." |
| **Mudajjan / müdeccel / müdeccan** | Ottoman terminology for the Morisco community. Used in official imperial documents. |
| **Andalusī / Andalusian** | Arabic/Ottoman term for Moriscos who had settled in the Maghreb after expulsion, denoting Iberian origin. |
| **Ehl-i Endülüs** | Ottoman term meaning "the people of al-Andalus," used to refer to Morisco communities. |
| **Taife-i müdeccel** | Ottoman term for the Morisco community as a collective group. |
| **New Christians** (*nuevos convertidos de moros*) | Official Spanish term for converted Muslims; also applied to converted Jews (*Judeoconversos*). |
| **Converso / Judeoconverso** | Descendant of Jews who converted to Christianity in late medieval and early modern Iberia. |
| **Crypto-Muslim** | A Morisco who publicly practised Christianity while secretly maintaining Islamic faith and practice. |
| **Marrano** | Pejorative term for a Christian suspected of secretly practising Judaism; applied by extension to crypto-religious communities generally. |
| **Renegade** | A Christian-born convert to Islam, often in the Ottoman or North African context. Kılıç Ali Pasha was a renegade. |
| **Sephardic Jews / Sephardim** | Jews of Iberian (Spanish and Portuguese) origin expelled in 1492 and 1497. |
| **Alfaquí** | Among Moriscos, the local religious leader (from Arabic *al-faqīh*, jurist); served as imam and preacher for crypto-Muslim communities. |
| **Dragoman** | Professional interpreter and cultural broker in the early modern Mediterranean world, especially at the Ottoman court and Venetian institutions. |

### Religion and Law

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Limpieza de sangre** | "Purity of blood" statutes in Spain; excluded those of Jewish or Muslim ancestry from certain offices and honours. Central mechanism of Morisco persecution. |
| **Taqiyya** | Islamic concept of permitted religious dissimulation when under coercion; provided theological justification for Morisco outward compliance with Christianity. |
| **Fetva / Fatwa** | A formal legal opinion or ruling issued by a qualified Islamic scholar. Morisco envoys petitioned the Ottoman Şeyhülislam for a fatwa declaring it the Sultan's duty to aid them. |
| **Şeyhülislam** | The highest religious authority (grand mufti) in the Ottoman Empire; key political figure approached by Morisco delegations. |
| **Inquisición / Inquisition** | The Spanish Inquisition; primary instrument of religious surveillance and persecution of Moriscos. |
| **Pragmática** | A Spanish royal decree with the force of law; Philip II's 1567 pragmática intensified cultural suppression of Moriscos. |
| **Reconquista** | The centuries-long Christian reconquest of the Iberian Peninsula from Muslim rule; ended with the fall of Granada in 1492. |
| **Capitulations of Granada** | The 1492 treaty ending the Reconquista; guaranteed freedom of religion to Granada's Muslims — a guarantee quickly violated. |
| **Confessionalization** | The early modern process by which states enforced religious orthodoxy and linked religious identity to political belonging. |
| **Jihad** | Holy war; the Morisco-Ottoman diplomatic discourse frequently invoked the language of jihad to frame appeals for Ottoman military intervention. |

### Ottoman Administration and Politics

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Sublime Porte** (*Bâb-ı Âlî*) | The office of the grand vizier; used in this dissertation to refer broadly to the Ottoman court and administration. |
| **Divan** | The Ottoman imperial council of state, presided over by the Grand Vizier. |
| **Grand Vizier** (*Sadrazam*) | The chief minister of the Ottoman Empire; the most powerful figure after the Sultan. |
| **Kapudan Pasha** | Grand Admiral of the Ottoman fleet; Kılıç Ali Pasha held this office 1571–1587. |
| **Beylerbeyi / Beylerbeylik** | Provincial governor / province in the Ottoman system. Algiers was the beylerbeylik of Cezayir-i Garb. |
| **Sancakbeyi** | Governor of a district (sancak) within an Ottoman province. |
| **Kapı** | Literally "gate/household"; the personal household and patronage network of an Ottoman grandee. Competing kapı networks shaped Ottoman foreign policy. |
| **Hükm** | An official imperial decree or judgment issued by the Ottoman Sultan. |
| **Arz** | A formal petition or report submitted to a ruler or high official in the Ottoman system. |
| **Mühimme Defterleri** | The Ottoman imperial registers recording important orders (*mühimme* = "important matters"). Key archival source for Morisco-Ottoman relations. |
| **Müteferrika** | A general Ottoman court title for individuals assigned to various duties; used here for Ottoman envoys of ambiguous status. |
| **Çelebi** | An Ottoman honorific meaning "gentleman" or "learned man." |
| **Müfti** | A religious scholar qualified to issue legal opinions; in European sources, often used for the Ottoman Şeyhülislam. |
| **Devşirme** | The Ottoman system of recruiting boys from Christian families (especially Balkan) for training in palace service and the military. Sokollu Mehmed Pasha was a devşirme. |

### Diplomatic and Analytical Terms

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Trans-imperial** | Spanning or crossing multiple empires; the dissertation's central analytical framework for Morisco mobility and relations. |
| **Trans-imperial subjects** | Individuals who crossed between empires and negotiated their political and social belonging across imperial boundaries. Coined by historian Natalie Rothman. |
| **Glocal / Glocalisation** | The interaction between global and local forces; how Morisco diplomacy operated simultaneously at local and trans-imperial scales. |
| **Bailo** | The Venetian ambassador to the Ottoman court; a key source for Mediterranean diplomatic history. |
| **Diaspora** | A community dispersed from its homeland; used for the Morisco communities scattered across the Mediterranean after expulsion. |
| **Cultural brokerage** | The practice of mediating between different cultural, linguistic, or religious worlds; a core Morisco strategy. |
| **Confessional boundary** | The religious frontier between Christianity and Islam; Moriscos strategically crossed these boundaries. |
| **In-betweenness** | The liminal condition of Moriscos who were neither fully Christian nor fully Muslim in the eyes of both worlds. A key analytical concept in the dissertation. |
| **Espionage / intelligence network** | System of covert information gathering; Moriscos operated within and across Ottoman and Spanish intelligence networks. |
| **Corsair** | A licensed pirate or privateer; a key figure in the early modern Mediterranean. Many Ottoman-Maghrebi captains who aided Moriscos (including Kılıç Ali Pasha's network) were former corsairs. |
| **Microhistory** | The historical method of focusing on small-scale case studies (individuals, communities) to illuminate broader structural patterns. |
| **Prosopography** | The systematic study of collective biographies; used in the dissertation for network analysis of Morisco agents. |
| **Longue durée** | Braudel's concept of long-duration historical processes; the dissertation situates Morisco-Ottoman relations within Mediterranean long-term structures. |

## Part IX: Website Tone and Presentation Recommendations

### Tone Guidance

The website portfolio should balance rigorous academic credibility with accessibility for the general public. Recommended register:

- **Scholarly but approachable:** Use precise terminology (listed above) with brief, clear definitions on first use. Avoid jargon for its own sake.
- **Mediterranean in scope:** Resist national narratives; the thesis is explicitly trans-imperial and pan-Mediterranean. Emphasise the interconnectedness of Spain, the Ottoman Empire, North Africa, Italy, and France.
- **Human-centred:** The dissertation is rich in individual stories — Morisco envoys travelling to Istanbul, a letter from Marseille to coreligionists in Istanbul hidden in fishing nets, a Morisco addressing the Venetian Doge in Spanish. Lead with these stories.
- **Contemporary resonance:** The thesis explicitly connects to present-day debates on migration, displacement, and identity. This link can be drawn carefully and tastefully on a public-facing site.
- **Visually grounded:** The subject is rich in cartographic and artistic materials. Maps, portraits, and paintings from the period can anchor sections and ground abstract concepts.

### Key Narrative Hooks for a General Audience

1. **The 1577 Istanbul Mission:** Morisco envoys from Valencia and Aragon arrive in Istanbul bearing gifts for the Grand Vizier, offering to fund an Ottoman fleet to sail along the coast of Valencia.
2. **The Marseille Letter:** A community of expelled Moriscos in Marseille writes to their representatives in Istanbul, passing news of violence against their community in Venice — the letter is then translated by a Morisco envoy and presented to the Venetian Doge.
3. **"Half Spanish, Half Turkish":** Venetian officials report Moriscos arriving in Istanbul dressed in mixed European and Ottoman dress — embodying the cultural in-betweenness at the heart of this history.
4. **The Ottoman Sultan's Letter to the Doge:** Sultan Ahmed I personally writes to Venice demanding protection for displaced Moriscos passing through Venetian territory — a displaced community leveraging imperial diplomacy.
5. **Hajı Ibrahim in Venice:** An Ottoman envoy of Morisco origin addresses the Venetian nobility directly in Spanish, without a translator — a single figure embodying three cultures at once.
