Dissertation

Moriscos and the Ottoman Empire: Entangled Histories in the Early Modern Mediterranean (1520–1620)

PhD dissertation · European University Institute, Florence · 2026

301 pages · 119,250 words · 7 maps · 11 archives

This dissertation reconstructs the entangled histories of the Morisco diaspora and the Ottoman Empire across the early modern Mediterranean, tracing Morisco diplomatic, espionage, and mobility strategies from the first Ottoman-Morisco contacts in the 1520s through the post-expulsion settlements of the 1620s. Drawing on primary sources in Spanish, Ottoman Turkish, Italian, French, Arabic, and Latin from eleven archives in four countries, it argues that the Moriscos were not passive victims of expulsion but active trans-imperial agents who deployed diplomacy, intelligence-gathering, mobility, and identity conversion as strategic tools for survival in a world defined by competing empires and confessional boundaries.

Chapter Structure

  1. Setting the Stage (1492–1568) — From Mudejars to Moriscos; the failure of early appeals to Morocco, Egypt, and Istanbul
  2. Between Empire and Rebellion (1568–1580) — The Alpujarras Rebellion; the 1577 Istanbul mission; Sokollu Mehmed Pasha and Kılıç Ali Pasha
  3. Networks of Trust: Spies and Brokers (1568–1609) — Morisco intelligence agents; Francisco Mariano; Jerónimo de Amiqui
  4. Crossing the Mediterranean (1609–1614) — Exile routes; Hajı Ibrahim Müteferrika in Venice; Ottoman diplomatic interventions
  5. A New Life in Istanbul (1609–1620s) — Galata settlement; Ahmad b. Qasim al-Hajarī; identity reformation after expulsion
  6. Settling the Stateless (1609–1640s) — Ottoman settlement policy; Salé-Rabat republic; diaspora across North Africa, Anatolia, and the Balkans
