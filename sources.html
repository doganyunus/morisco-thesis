Methodology

Trans-Imperial Approach

The dissertation adopts a trans-imperial framework that situates Morisco history within the overlapping and competing structures of the Habsburg Spanish and Ottoman empires. Rather than studying the Moriscos from within a single national history, the research follows their networks across imperial frontiers — tracing the same individuals, families, and letters through Spanish, Italian, Ottoman, and French archives simultaneously.

The trans-imperial framework allows the dissertation to capture what no single national archive can reveal: the way the same Morisco agent might appear as a spy in the Spanish papeles de estado, a petitioner in the Ottoman Mühimme Defterleri, and a refugee in the Venetian bailo dispatches — simultaneously, in the same year.

Microhistory

Alongside the structural analysis, the dissertation uses microhistorical close reading to reconstruct individual lives. The movements of Francisco Mariano (spy), Jerónimo Enríquez (Marseille procurator), and Ahmad b. Qasim al-Hajarī (scholar-diplomat) are traced in granular detail through bureaucratic paper trails, allowing the larger patterns of Morisco experience to emerge from specific, documented cases.

The microhistorical method is particularly suited to populations who left few first-person sources: by reading against the grain of administrative, inquisitorial, and diplomatic records, the dissertation recovers voices that official narratives sought to suppress or ignore.

Digital Humanities

The dissertation integrates digital humanities tools at two levels: network analysis (using Gephi) to map the relationships between Morisco agents, Ottoman patrons, and European intermediaries; and geospatial mapping (using Carto) to visualise Morisco migration routes and settlement patterns across the Mediterranean. Seven maps appear in the dissertation.

Network analysis allows the dissertation to visualise the structure of the Morisco intelligence community — identifying the most connected brokers, the structural holes in communication networks, and the nodes through which information flowed between Istanbul, Madrid, and Venice.

Types of Sources

01 Diplomatic Correspondence

Ambassadors' reports, imperial orders, letters between courts. The Venetian bailo dispatches (Senato Dispacci Costantinopoli) and Spanish papeles de estado provide the richest documentation of Morisco-Ottoman contacts. These sources capture the Morisco question as it appeared to contemporaries: as a matter of imperial strategy, religious policy, and cross-Mediterranean intelligence.

02 Inquisitorial Records

Trial sentences and proceeding records from the Spanish Inquisition. While biased sources that must be read critically against their institutional purposes, they preserve uniquely detailed information about crypto-Islamic practices, personal networks, and identities that Moriscos themselves left no direct record of. The Inquisition's obsessive documentation of deviation is, paradoxically, the richest source for understanding Morisco religious and cultural life.

03 Ottoman Imperial Registers

The Mühimme Defterleri record imperial orders concerning all manner of state business, including Moriscos. These entries appear rarely but are decisive when they do — proving that Morisco affairs reached the highest levels of Ottoman government and were considered worthy of imperial attention, not merely the informal concern of individual grandees like Sokollu Mehmed Pasha or Kılıç Ali Pasha.

04 Chronicles and Travel Accounts

Luis del Mármol Carvajal's Historia del rebelión y castigo de los moriscos del reino de Granada (1600); Evliya Çelebi's Seyahatnâme; Mustafa Selaniki's Tarih-i Selaniki. These narrative sources provide essential context for the broader events within which the archival evidence is situated, and occasionally preserve information — particularly about the Ottoman side — not available in the diplomatic record.

05 Morisco Intellectual Writings

Ahmad b. Qasim al-Hajarī's Kitab Nasir al-Din ʿala al-Qawm al-Kafirin (Book of the Protector of Religion against the Unbelievers); Aljamiado manuscripts from the Biblioteca Nacional de España. These rare first-person Morisco voices are treated with particular care — as documents produced under conditions of extreme constraint that both reveal and conceal, offering indirect access to the subjective experience of displacement and identity-making.

People and Communities

Morisco
Former Muslim of the Iberian Peninsula forcibly converted to Christianity between 1499 and 1526. The term was in common use by the mid-sixteenth century and is retained in the dissertation as the standard historical designation.
Mudéjar
A Muslim who lived as a subject of a Christian kingdom before forced conversions; from Arabic mudajjan, "permitted to remain." The Mudejar period ended with the forced conversions of 1499–1526.
Mudajjan / müdeccel / müdeccan
Ottoman terminology for the Morisco community; transliterations of the Arabic root meaning "permitted to remain." Ottoman documents use these terms to designate Moriscos as a recognised, named community.
Andalusī / Andalusian
Arabic and Ottoman term for Moriscos settled in the Maghreb after expulsion; referring to their Iberian (al-Andalus) origin. Still used in North Africa today to describe communities of Andalusi descent.
Ehl-i Endülüs
Ottoman term meaning "the people of al-Andalus"; used in imperial correspondence to designate expelled Moriscos seeking protection and settlement.
Taife-i müdeccel
Ottoman term for the Morisco community as a collective group; taife denotes a community or group; used in the Mühimme Defterleri.
New Christians (nuevos convertidos de moros)
Official Spanish term for converted Muslims; distinguished from conversos (converted Jews) and subject to specific Inquisitorial surveillance.
Converso / Judeoconverso
Descendant of Jews who converted to Christianity, many under duress in 1391 or 1492; subject to similar suspicions and limpieza de sangre exclusions as Moriscos.
Crypto-Muslim
A Morisco who publicly practised Christianity while secretly maintaining Islamic faith and practice. The Inquisition's obsessive concern with crypto-Islam generated the documentary record on which much of this dissertation depends.
Renegade
A Christian-born convert to Islam in the Ottoman or North African context; Kılıç Ali Pasha (born Giovanni Dionigi Galeni in Calabria) is the most prominent example in this dissertation. Renegades and Moriscos occupied analogous positions of bicultural ambiguity.
Sephardic Jews / Sephardim
Jews of Iberian origin expelled from Spain in 1492 and Portugal in 1497; many settled in the Ottoman Empire, especially Salonica and Istanbul's Galata district — the same neighbourhoods where Morisco exiles would later arrive.
Alfaquí
Among Moriscos, the local religious leader who served as imam for crypto-Muslim communities; the carrier of Aljamiado manuscripts and Islamic legal knowledge; a key target of Inquisitorial prosecution.
Dragoman
Professional interpreter and cultural broker in the early modern Mediterranean; Moriscos frequently served in this capacity at both Ottoman and European courts, leveraging their linguistic dexterity.

Religion and Law

Limpieza de sangre
"Purity of blood" statutes excluding those of Jewish or Muslim ancestry from certain offices, institutions, and honours; the core legal mechanism of Morisco exclusion from Spanish civil and religious society.
Taqiyya
Islamic concept of permitted religious dissimulation under coercion; cited by North African jurists (in fatwas, including the Oran Fatwa of c. 1504) as justification for Moriscos' outward Christian practice while maintaining inner Muslim faith.
Fetva / Fatwa
A formal legal opinion issued by a qualified Islamic scholar; the Oran Fatwa (c. 1504) is particularly important for legitimising Morisco crypto-Islam. The Şeyhülislam's opinions on Morisco reconversions after 1609 are also consulted.
Şeyhülislam
The highest religious authority (grand mufti) in the Ottoman Empire; responsible for issuing fatwas on the status of Morisco Muslims and the legitimacy of their return to Islam after compelled apostasy.
Inquisición / Inquisition
The Spanish Inquisition; the primary instrument of Morisco persecution and, paradoxically, the primary producer of documentation about Morisco life. Its records at the AHN and ARV are central sources for this dissertation.
Pragmática
A Spanish royal decree; Philip II's 1567 pragmática banned the Arabic language, Morisco dress, bathing practices, and cultural customs — directly precipitating the Alpujarras Rebellion of 1568.
Reconquista
The Christian military reconquest of the Iberian Peninsula from Muslim rulers, completed with the fall of Granada in 1492. The Reconquista framework shaped the ideological justifications for Morisco persecution and expulsion.
Capitulations of Granada
The 1492 treaty between the Catholic Monarchs and Muhammad XII guaranteeing freedom of religion, property, and customs to Granada's Muslim population; broken by Cardinal Cisneros within seven years.
Confessionalization
The early modern process linking religious identity to political belonging and state formation; the Moriscos were direct victims of its Spanish Habsburg implementation, which demanded religious uniformity as a precondition of political loyalty.
Aljamiado
Spanish (or occasionally Aragonese) written in Arabic script; the literary medium of Morisco crypto-Islamic culture; manuscripts held in the BNE are among the most important first-person Morisco sources available.

Ottoman Administration

Sublime Porte (Bâb-ı Âlî)
The office of the grand vizier; broadly, the central Ottoman court and government; used as a metonym for the Ottoman state in diplomatic correspondence.
Divan
The Ottoman imperial council of state; met four times weekly; the arena for Ottoman foreign policy decisions regarding Moriscos and other external matters.
Grand Vizier (Sadrazam)
Chief minister of the Ottoman Empire; Sokollu Mehmed Pasha (1565–1579) is the key figure in this dissertation, having received the Morisco delegations of 1577.
Kapudan Pasha
Grand Admiral of the Ottoman fleet; Kılıç Ali Pasha held this office from 1571 to 1587 and was the principal Ottoman naval commander and personal link to Morisco petitioners.
Kapı
Household and patronage network of an Ottoman grandee; Morisco envoys sought to enter the kapı of Sokollu Mehmed Pasha and Kılıç Ali Pasha as a means of securing imperial protection.
Mühimme Defterleri
Ottoman imperial registers recording important (mühimme) orders; held at the BOA; abbreviated MD. The Mühimme entries on Morisco settlers are rare but decisive evidence of Ottoman state engagement with the Morisco question at the highest level.
Devşirme
The Ottoman system of recruiting boys from Christian families for imperial service; both Sokollu Mehmed Pasha and Kılıç Ali Pasha entered Ottoman service through related conversion processes, making them natural interlocutors for Morisco converts.
Beylerbeyi / Beylerbeylik
Provincial governor / province in the Ottoman system; the beylerbeyis of Algiers and Tunis were key intermediaries between the Ottoman centre and Morisco communities arriving in North Africa.
Hükm
An official imperial decree issued by the Ottoman Sultan; several hükms concerning Morisco settlers and their rights are recorded in the Mühimme Defterleri.

Diplomatic and Analytical Terms

Trans-imperial
Spanning or crossing multiple empires; the dissertation's central analytical framework. Trans-imperial history follows subjects, networks, and texts as they cross imperial frontiers, rather than reading a period through the lens of a single empire or nation-state.
Trans-imperial subjects
Individuals who crossed between empires, negotiating belonging across boundaries; Francisco Mariano and Jerónimo de Amiqui are exemplary cases — Moriscos who simultaneously operated within the Habsburg and Ottoman intelligence worlds.
Bailo
The Venetian ambassador to the Ottoman court; the bailo's dispatches (Senato Dispacci Costantinopoli) are among the most detailed surviving records of Ottoman court life and constitute a major source for Morisco-Ottoman relations.
Cultural brokerage
Mediating between different cultural, linguistic, or religious worlds; Moriscos were among the most effective cultural brokers in the early modern Mediterranean, leveraging their bicultural competencies as a resource for survival.
In-betweenness
The liminal condition of Moriscos: neither fully accepted as Christians in Spain nor fully trusted as Muslims in Ottoman lands. The dissertation argues that their strength lay precisely in this ambiguity, which enabled movement and negotiation across confessional boundaries.
Corsair
A licensed pirate or privateer operating in the early modern Mediterranean; key figure in the political economy of the sea. Kılıç Ali Pasha began his career as a corsair; the corsair networks of Algiers and Tunis were crucial conduits for Morisco communication with the Ottoman world.
Microhistory
Historical method focusing on small-scale, densely documented case studies to illuminate larger structural patterns; associated with Carlo Ginzburg (The Cheese and the Worms) and Giovanni Levi (Inheriting Power). Used in the dissertation to reconstruct individual Morisco lives from bureaucratic paper trails.
Prosopography
Systematic study of collective biographies through the aggregation of individual cases; used for network analysis of Morisco agents and their Ottoman and European contacts in Chapters III and IV.
Longue durée
Fernand Braudel's concept of long-duration historical processes operating beneath the level of events; applied here to the deep structures of Mediterranean connectivity that made Morisco trans-imperial mobility possible.
Diaspora
A community dispersed from its homeland while maintaining collective memory, cultural identity, and often aspirations of return; the Morisco diaspora is the largest single forced displacement in early modern European history.
